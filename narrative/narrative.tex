\section{Introduction}
Ximera, pronounced ``chimera,'' (\textbf{X}imera:
\textbf{I}nteractive, \textbf{M}athematics, \textbf{E}ducation,
\textbf{R}esources, for \textbf{A}ll) is an open-source platform that
provides tools for authoring and publishing (PDF and Online),
open-source, interactive educational content, such as
textbooks, assessments, and online courses.  The Ximera team supports the development of the Ximera platform, educational materials, and instructor tools. Through this proposal, we seek support to continue and expand our work in all three areas. \textbf{The ultimate goal of this project is to promote sustained student success and savings.}

\paragraph{Past Awards and Current Funding} 

The Ximera Project is funded 2024--2026 (with no other external funding) by a \$2,125,000 Open Textbooks Pilot Program grant. We will refer to this as OTP Phase I funding. In the past, the Ximera Project has also received support from NSF Grant DUE-1245433 (\$180,000), the Shuttleworth Foundation (\$5,000), the Affordable Learning Exchange at OSU (\$16,000), and the Ohio State University Department of Mathematics.




The Ximera platform has three basic types of users: Students, instructors, and authors. We will discuss each in turn below.

\paragraph{Students Find Success Though Ximera}

While affordability is an important benefit, student success remains the primary goal of the project.
Authors and instructors use Ximera because of its flexibility \cite{active-learning-osu}. Ximera 
empowers university instructors to tailor content based on the
specific needs of their students, enhancing student engagement and
understanding.  

Moreover, Ximera's ability to provide backup materials in the form of
PDFs of the online course materials ensures continuity of learning
even in the face of technical difficulties or internet disruptions
\cite{active-learning-osu}.  

Quantitative work performed by Ohio State researchers also supports the
effectiveness of Ximera for student success.  Specifically,
researchers analyzed the correlation between the total number of
learning events for a student and their exam scores
\cite{ximera-calculus}. The study found a significant relationship
between increased learning events and improved exam performance. More
specifically, a simple linear regression was used to predict a
student's total exam score based on the logarithm of the number of
learning events recorded for that student. The results revealed a
positive correlation, suggesting that higher engagement, as measured
by learning events, contributes to improved academic outcomes. These
findings highlight the potential of Ximera to enhance student success
in mathematics courses.
Together, these findings suggest that Ximera advances both components of the project's mission: reducing educational costs while supporting student success.


\subsection{Instructor Experience}

Instructors (who are not authors) can freely use any Ximera materials simply by accessing the materials' URL. Moreover, if an instructor encounters issues with the content or platform performance, there are straightforward ways to report them. Since Ximera is built using GitHub (see Section~\ref{ss:gh}), we use GitHub's issue-tracking system to collect and manage feedback.

Because the content is open and written in \LaTeX\ (see Section~\ref{ss:LaTeX}), a language familiar to most mathematics instructors, users can also submit corrections and improvements directly through GitHub. As one example, instructors using The Ohio State University's calculus sequence have identified and helped resolve 419 issues in the course materials.

As Ximera authors create more materials with interactive features in mind, instructors increasingly have options to incorporate discussions, group work, labs, and other forms of interactive learning into their classrooms. For example, Differential Equations utilizes spreadsheet labs that include embedded videos and links to sample spreadsheets; Linear Algebra includes many geometric explorations and links to coding exercises, while Precalculus by Stitz and Zeager, currently being converted to Ximera, boasts connections between the concrete and the abstract that can be used for classroom discussion or to encourage independent study. From a technical perspective, instructor use of Ximera falls into three categories:

\paragraph{Online Materials with LMS Integration} At 
OSU and UF, instructors of large classes and a few others have Ximera
assignments directly integrated into the Learning Management System
(LMS). For these instructors, Ximera works seamlessly and invisibly.

\paragraph{Online Materials without LMS Integration}
At other institutions using Ximera, instructors can use Ximera
materials, and have their students complete various Ximera
assignments. However, data access is prohibitively difficult for most
instructors in this category.

\paragraph{PDF Materials}
There are a number of Ximera courses that are not yet deployed
online. Their materials are currently only in PDF form. Instructors in
this category are usually in a development phase of their Ximera
course. The PDFs produced allow incremental development of the
materials while still being used and tested in the classroom.

\subsection{Exceptional Design Elements in Ximera}

Ximera is built by educators, for educators. Three design elements distinguish the platform and contribute to its effectiveness for students, instructors, and authors.

\paragraph{Simultaneous Output}
Ximera allows authors to maintain a single source file that generates both PDF and interactive online content. This approach supports flexible pedagogy, accommodates different learning preferences, and provides a reliable backup format when internet or technical issues arise.

\paragraph{Open Community}
Ximera content is openly licensed and developed through public GitHub repositories. This model promotes transparency, collaboration, and continuous improvement while allowing institutions and instructors to adapt materials to their local needs. In addition, all published content is freely accessible without login requirements, supporting the project's commitment to open education.

\paragraph{Immediate Feedback}
Ximera's interactive exercises provide students with immediate feedback as they work. By allowing students to identify and correct mistakes in real time, these tools promote engagement, persistence, and deeper learning.

\subsection{Technical Details}
\label{ss:LaTeX}
The technical infrastructure described below currently supports tens of thousands of enrolled students annually and millions of public visits to Ximera-hosted content.

Ximera uses \LaTeX\ as its authoring language. \LaTeX\ is a markup language created in the 1980s. It provides a
simplified way to produce documents by separating the content and
structure from the formatting. \LaTeX\ is particularly useful for
producing complex documents, such as books, reports, and academic
papers. It also has a large number of packages and extensions that can
be used to customize the output and automate many tasks.



Authors write their content in \LaTeX\ using the Ximera document
class, which includes formatting for both PDF and online formats.
Within the \LaTeX\ document, authors can use various Ximera macros to
add interactive content such as answer boxes and embedded videos or
graphs.  Authors then (invisibly) compile their document and generate the necessary files for both
PDF and online versions of their content (see Figure~\ref{F:XG}).

\begin{figure}
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.8\textwidth]{XimeraGraphic.png}
  \caption{Ximera allows an author to simultaneously write traditional
    and online interactive content.}
  \label{F:XG}
\end{figure}

Once the content is complete and compiled to HTML and JavaScript,
authors push their work to a Ximera server. Students can then access
the online content and interact with the various interactive elements
as designed by the author. All interaction is happening on the
student's own computer.  



\paragraph{\LaTeX\ Documentclass}
In \LaTeX, a \emph{document class} is a file that specifies the overall
layout and formatting of a document. It defines the structure of the
document, including elements such as the title page, headers, footers,
and the document body. The Ximera document class provides the
following features:

\begin{description}
\item[Formatting for different domains] The Ximera document class
  provides built-in support for formatting documents in both PDF and
  online formats, which can be a big time-saver for
  authors. Additionally, it allows for the simultaneous creation of
  solution manuals and teaching editions, which can be especially
  useful for educators.
\item[Compiling individually or as a whole] With the Ximera document
  class, authors can easily compile individual documents or an entire
  collection of documents. This flexibility can be helpful when making
  changes to specific documents without having to re-compile the
  entire collection. Moreover, this allows an author to share large
  portions of a text with another, with minimal changes.
\item[Interactive content] The Ximera document class allows for the
  inclusion of interactive content, such as answer boxes that are
  validated by a client-side computer algebra system. Additionally, it
  allows for the embedding of YouTube videos, Desmos graphs, and
  GeoGebra interactives. 
\item[All content displayed] By default, the Ximera document class
  displays all content to the author. This means the author sees what
  the students see, along with answers and solutions, and links (that
  can be checked) to various interactive elements (when deployed, the
  interactive elements are truly embedded). This can be especially
  helpful for catching errors or inconsistencies in the content.
\end{description}

\paragraph{Answer Verification and Computer Algebra System}

\textit{Math-expressions} is a JavaScript library that can be used both
client-side and server-side for parsing and working with mathematical
expressions.
%%  It provides functionality for basic computer algebra,
%% such as symbolic differentiation and numerically identifying
%% equivalent expressions. The library allows users to create,
%% manipulate, and compare mathematical expressions in text and \LaTeX\
%% formats.
%%
 Ximera uses the math-expressions library for answer validation. In the
context of Ximera, the library helps parse and evaluate student input,
making it possible to check if their answers are correct or equivalent
to the expected solutions.
%% The math-expressions library enables Ximera
%% to handle a wide range of mathematical expressions, including those
%% involving functions, variables, and complex operations. 


To validate that an answer is correct, some sort of heuristic is
required. This is due to Richardson's theorem. This theorem says that
equality is undecidable between functions defined by expressions
involving exponential and trigonometric functions and standard
constants \cite{richardson}. This means that in theory (and in
practice), a computer algebra system cannot always validate correct
answers as correct, and incorrect answers as incorrect.  Hence
Math-expressions uses a few different heuristics for answer
validation.



%% The most important relies on complex numbers:
%% math-expressions uses complex numbers in answer validation by
%% generating random complex number bindings for variables, evaluating
%% expressions with these bindings, and comparing their numerical
%% equivalence within specified tolerances.  This results in a quite
%% robust system for answer validation.

There are precedents to this system.  Some of the techniques are
summarized by Chris Sangwin \cite{sangwin}, with a popular standard
technique being to check for equality at random \textit{real} points
\cite{fisher}.


On the other hand, math-expressions uses complex numbers to validate
answers. This seems to work quite well.  One metric is the STACK
project \cite{stack}, which has produced a wide variety of pairs of
expressions which should be adjudicated as equal, and pairs which
should be adjudicated as different.  These form the basis for unit
tests of math-expressions.  Out of more than five thousand test cases,
math-expressions fails on just a handful.

\paragraph{Modulus: LMS Integration}

Setting up a Ximera activity within a Learning Managment System (LMS) like Canvas or Blackboard currently means using a Learning Tools Interoperability (LTI) link. LTI is a standard protocol that allows different learning applications to communicate and work together. One of the outcomes of our current work is the development of a new LTI~1.3 connection via ``Modulus,'' a novel assignment-grade database. This new connection provides a seamless and efficient experience for both instructors and students.

Modulus is being developed by Infonomic under the direction of Snapp and Fowler. At the time of this writing, it is being tested in test-instances of OSU's Canvas installation. Modulus will allow instructors to easily create and manage Ximera assignments within their LMS, while also providing a centralized database for tracking student progress and performance. This integration will enable instructors to access detailed analytics and insights about student engagement and learning outcomes, ultimately supporting the goal of promoting sustained student success. \paragraph{Open Licensing}
For Open Educational Resources (OER) to achieve their full potential,
it is key that not only the content be openly licensed, but also that
the platforms and underlying technologies that support this content be
similarly licensed.%%  This is fundamental to ensure that OER is not only
%% freely accessible but also freely adaptable and reusable, ultimately
%% upholding the spirit of openness in education.

Ximera is committed to open licensing. The content on Ximera is
generally licensed under Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike (CC
BY-SA). This license allows others to remix, tweak, and build upon our
work even for commercial purposes, as long as they credit us and
license their new creations under the identical terms. This promotes
the creation of a rich, diverse, and dynamic pool of educational
resources that supports and enhances learning experiences.  However,
it's noteworthy that some content within our platform is licensed
under Creative Commons Attribution-NonCommercial-ShareAlike (CC
BY-NC-SA). This essentially allows for adaptation and building upon
the work non-commercially, with new works licensed under the same
terms. When we adopt such content, we must maintain the NC
license. This choice, while limiting commercial uses, ensures the
content remains free for educational and other non-profit purposes.

Again, not just the content but also the technology that powers Ximera
subscribes to the ethos of openness. The \LaTeX\ code powering Ximera,
like the ``documentclass'' are licensed under the \LaTeX\ Project
Public License (LPPL). This license, which governs most of \LaTeX\
itself, ensures that the components of the system remain freely
available, modifiable, and distributable. It supports the continuous
improvement and adaptation of the system, contributing to the quality
and flexibility of the resources we can offer.

The code that powers the Ximera server is currently licensed under the
General Public License (GPL). This not only allows users to run the
program for any purpose but also provides the freedom to study how the
program works, and change it to make it do what they wish. Modulus is licensed under the
Affero General Public License (AGPL). The AGPL is better suited for
networked services like Modulus as it ensures that even if the software
is running on a server, users have access to its source code. This
enhances transparency and promotes collaborative improvements. By
adopting the AGPL, we will ensure that, in the future, Ximera and Modulus remain
a truly open platform, dedicated to facilitating access to
high-quality educational resources for all.

\paragraph{Modulus Atlas}\label{par:modulus-atlas} Discovery is the central bottleneck for any

\section{Absolute Priority 2: Addressing Gaps in the Open Textbook Marketplace and Bringing Solutions to Scale}
% Comprehensive plan covering all six elements:
\subsection{Assessment of Existing Open Educational Resources}   % (a)

Before proposing new development, we assessed the existing open
textbook marketplace at two levels: the architectural level (how
content is delivered and how interactivity scales) and the validation
level (how student responses are checked, relative to industry-leading
commercial platforms).

\paragraph{Architectural gaps} At the architectural level, the
current open textbook marketplace shows five recurring gaps: limited
interactive or dynamic content, with many free and open textbooks
restricted to static PDFs or web pages; a lack of integrated online
homework systems, forcing educators who want a free textbook paired
with free homework to choose one or the other; limited content
customization, since most online textbooks do not allow an instructor
to adapt material to their own students; inconsistent ADA compliance
across the marketplace as a whole, leaving many free and open
resources inaccessible to some students with disabilities (a gap
Ximera itself has only recently closed through the accessibility work
described under Absolute Priority~3); and, most consequentially for
scale, a near-universal reliance on server-side interactivity.
Table~\ref{tab:calc-comparison} summarizes this last gap among the
leading free calculus textbooks.

Server-side interactivity requires a central server to mediate every
student interaction. When the number of students attempting to
interact with that server grows large, server-side solutions become
difficult and expensive to scale; Active Calculus, the only one of
these peer texts to offer dynamic content at all, does so through a
server-side architecture. Ximera, by contrast, performs all
interactivity, answer checking, graphing, and feedback on the
student's own computer (Section~\ref{ss:LaTeX}); the central server's
role is limited to serving static content. This is why Ximera has been
able to support MOOCs with tens of thousands of simultaneous
enrollments and OSU's large coordinated calculus sections without the
server-capacity problems that constrain server-side competitors. At
OSU and UF, Ximera-deployed courses already meet or exceed every other
open resource across all five categories in
Table~\ref{tab:calc-comparison}; the task this project addresses is
bringing that same standard to every Ximera course, at every
consortium institution.

\paragraph{Validation-level gaps} Beyond architecture, we conducted a
systematic comparison of Ximera's answer-validation and authoring
capabilities against the leading commercial mathematics homework
platforms. Here by `validator' we mean a given online homework system's ability to detect a correct answer from an incorrect answer and vice versa. we evaluated each platform: Pearson MyLab Math, McGraw Hill ALEKS, Cengage WebAssign,
Hawkes Learning, and MyOpenMath/IMathAS. Seven dimensions were analyzed: standard response validation, algebraic and
functional submissions, graphing, randomization, factored-form
validation, targeted feedback, and custom-validation authoring.

For ordinary response types, numeric entry, text entry, multiple
choice, and select-all, the major platforms are broadly comparable,
and Ximera's support is on par with industry leaders. The more
consequential differences emerge in mathematically richer territory.
Ximera's default algebraic validator is unusually robust because its
equivalence check samples many complex-plane points, motivated by the
identity theorem, rather than relying only on a small fixed set of
real-number substitutions.


Ximera also offers additional flexibility by exposing an optional
author-written JavaScript validation pathway as well as one using the open-source computer algebra system SAGE.
These powerful tools allow Ximera to validate novel mathematical forms that simply would not be possible with other systems.

\subsection{Review Protocols: Accuracy, Rigor, and Accessibility} % (c)

Authors make errors. However, 
Because content is hosted on GitHub
(Section~\ref{ss:gh}), errors are tracked as issues, and can be corrected transparently, with a visible history of
revision. Flash Grants frequently fund targeted review work, for
example, reviewing or editing one-third of an existing online course,
and completed Flash Grant deliverables are checked against their
proposed scope before stipends are paid.

Accessibility review is conducted by Jeff Kuan of BAWL (Botting
Accessibility with \LaTeX), the project's accessibility specialist,
who audits new and existing content for WCAG~2.1 Level~AA compliance,
maintains the accessibility sections of the Ximera User Manual, and

award, will continue and expand under this grant, with an explicit
goal of training consortium authors directly in accessible authoring
practices so that accessibility review becomes distributed across the
consortium rather than centralized in a single specialist.

\subsection{Dissemination and Adoption Beyond the Consortium}    % (d)

Dissemination beyond the consortium is addressed primarily through
\emph{Modulus Atlas} (Section~\ref{par:modulus-atlas}), the discovery
and dissemination platform described under Absolute Priority~1, which
automatically surfaces newly registered Ximera activities to the
public, independent of whether the registering institution is a
formal consortium member. Modulus Atlas is modeled on Uitwiskeling
(\url{https://www.uitwiskeling.be}), a Flemish mathematics journal and
portal that Ximera has already partnered with through a Flash Grant,
expanding Ximera's reach into Belgian K-12 schools entirely outside
the consortium's original footprint. This precedent demonstrates that
dissemination through curated, discoverable publication can drive
adoption well beyond formal consortium institutions, and Modulus Atlas
is designed to generalize that pattern automatically and at scale. As
described under Absolute Priority~1, we will further extend
interoperability outward to PreTeXt, STACK, and WeBWorK once Ximera
activities are fully instrumented in Modulus, multiplying reach across
the broader OER ecosystem rather than limiting dissemination to
institutions with a direct relationship to the project.

We will also continue established outreach channels: exhibitor booths
and minicourses at major mathematics meetings (the Joint Mathematics
Meetings, MAA MathFest, AMATYC, and others), the Ximera Office Hours
and Virtual Support Desk, and direct outreach by Paul Zachlin, our
Dissemination Specialist, to identify new conference and institutional
opportunities throughout the year.

\section{Statement of Need}

While open educational resources have demonstrated their potential to reduce costs and improve access, significant barriers remain between the existence of OER and their successful adoption at scale. Many high-quality resources remain difficult to discover, are insufficiently open,  or insufficiently supported for LMS intgeration. At the same time, faculty often lack the time, technical support, and incentives necessary to identify, adapt, and deploy OER effectively.


Ximera does a good job of solving the problem above. It uses a language (\LaTeX) that faculty are familier with. Modulus will ensure LMS integration. However, the real issue is that Ximera is truly free and open, we have no way of knowing who uses Ximera unless they directly contact us. Moreover, others have no way of finding our work, unless we publish direct links to it in.
\textbf{Hence, the challenge is not simply the creation of additional content. It is ensuring that existing and newly developed resources are discoverable, maintainable, reliable, and capable of supporting student success over time. }



This need is particularly acute in high-enrollment courses, where even modest barriers to adoption can affect thousands of students. To maximize student savings and educational impact, the consortium must provide both high-quality open resources and the infrastructure necessary for their successful dissemination, implementation, and continuous improvement.





\section{Project Design}

The consortium will promote student success through a comprehensive strategy that combines open resource adoption, implementation support, continuous improvement, and long-term sustainability.

\subsection{Flash Grants for Local Adoption and Improvement}

To accelerate adoption across consortium institutions, the project will establish a competitive Flash Grant program. Small, rapid-turnaround awards will provide faculty and departments with targeted support to adopt, adapt, or expand the use of Modulus-enabled open educational resources in high-enrollment courses.

Flash Grants will support activities such as course redesign, resource adaptation, accessibility improvements, assessment development, and implementation evaluation. By reducing the local costs of adoption, the program will expand the use of open resources while generating new examples, adaptations, and evidence that can be shared across the consortium.

\subsection{Modulus Atlas: Discovery, Dissemination, and Sustainability}

The project will develop Modulus Atlas, an open discovery and dissemination platform that serves as the public-facing hub of the Modulus ecosystem. Atlas will provide a searchable directory of Modulus-instrumented learning activities organized by academic discipline and educational sector, enabling instructors to identify relevant resources and implementation models.

Atlas will integrate with the existing Modulus registry infrastructure to automatically surface newly registered activities, helping ensure that innovations developed through the project remain visible and reusable. The platform will also host documentation, implementation guides, case studies, and evidence of impact, reducing barriers to adoption and strengthening long-term sustainability. By transforming discovery from a manual process into an ongoing platform function, Atlas will support continued growth and use of project resources well beyond the grant period.

\subsection{Expanding and Integrating Existing OER}

Consistent with the Open Textbook Pilot requirement to assess and build upon existing resources before creating new ones, the consortium will systematically identify, evaluate, and integrate high-quality OER from across the broader educational ecosystem. Rather than duplicating existing work, the project will prioritize adaptation, enhancement, and interoperability wherever appropriate.

Existing open textbooks, ancillary materials, simulations, assessments, and learning activities will be reviewed for alignment with project objectives and incorporated into the Modulus ecosystem when suitable. Resources integrated through the project will be enhanced with Modulus instrumentation, accessibility improvements, implementation guidance, and evidence-based instructional supports. This approach maximizes efficiency, accelerates adoption, and expands the reach of proven open resources while creating a more cohesive ecosystem for faculty and students.

\subsection{Monitoring Impact and Improving Student Outcomes}

The consortium will use Modulus analytics, institutional data, and implementation reporting to track adoption, student savings, instructional practices, and learning outcomes across participating institutions. Measures will include resource utilization, course completion, withdrawal rates, academic performance, faculty adoption, and direct student cost savings.

Findings will be used to identify evidence-based practices associated with successful OER implementation and student achievement. These practices will be documented through Atlas and disseminated through consortium workshops, professional development activities, publications, and conference presentations.

\subsection{Sustainability Beyond the Grant Period}

Sustainability is built directly into the project design. Modulus Atlas provides a durable dissemination infrastructure, while the Flash Grant program establishes a replicable model for supporting ongoing local innovation. By combining discoverability, implementation support, evidence generation, and community engagement, the project creates a self-reinforcing ecosystem that will continue to expand the adoption and impact of open educational resources after federal funding concludes.

Through these integrated activities, the project will achieve substantial student cost savings, increase adoption of open educational resources in high-enrollment courses, improve educational outcomes, and create a sustainable framework for continued growth and innovation.

section{Management Plan}

Responsibility for each activity area below rests with a named lead,
backed by a specific contract or personnel line in the budget. Snapp,
as Project Director, holds quarterly check-ins with each lead; these
feed directly into the annual APR cycle and give the project an early
warning mechanism for schedule slippage, the lesson learned from the
six-month delay in contracting Infonomic under the current award.

\subsection{Dissemination}

Dissemination is led jointly by Snapp and Paul Zachlin (Lakeland
Community College), who continues as Dissemination Specialist under a
\$5,000/year contract (\$20,000 over four years), supported by a
\$46,000/year domestic travel budget (\$184,000 total) covering
exhibitor booths and presentations at the Joint Mathematics Meetings,
MAA MathFest, AMATYC, and similar venues. Zachlin is responsible for
identifying new conference and institutional outreach opportunities
throughout the year and reports quarterly to Snapp on contacts made,
presentations delivered, and leads converted to adoption. Beyond
direct outreach, \emph{Modulus Atlas} (Section~\ref{ss:modulus-atlas})
functions as a standing, automated dissemination channel once
deployed, surfacing new Ximera activities publicly without requiring
staff time per institution. Progress is measured annually using the
same metrics established under the current award: institutions and
individuals contacted, presentations given, and rate of adoption,
reported in the APR alongside Net Promoter Score data.

\subsection{Professional Development}

The annual Ximera Workshop remains the primary professional
development vehicle, organized by Snapp and Fowler with Wim Obbels
(DevOps, \$12,000/year contract) and Jeff Kuan (accessibility,
Section~\ref{ss:accessibility}) leading hands-on sessions on server
infrastructure, validators, and accessible authoring. Workshop and
ongoing-support costs are drawn from the Materials and Supplies line
(\$6,000 in Year 1, \$2,000 in Years 2--4) and the general travel
budget. Between workshops, professional development continues through
monthly Ximera Office Hours and a Virtual Support Desk, both staffed
by existing personnel at no additional cost. Milestone: at least one
multi-day workshop held each year, with attendance and post-workshop
authoring activity tracked as a professional-development performance
measure in the APR.

\subsection{Flash Grants}

Flash Grants are administered by Snapp and Fowler, who review proposed
scopes of work and approve stipends ranging from \$400 to \$4,000,
budgeted at \$54,000/year (\$216,000 total), supporting approximately
32 awards annually. Awards are paid only upon verified completion of
the agreed scope, a mechanism that has already proven effective:
under the current FIPSE award, approximately 80 Flash Grants were
awarded at this rate, contributing directly to growth from 10 to 15
adopting institutions. Under this grant, the same review-and-pay-on-
completion structure continues, with Snapp confirming deliverables
against the original scope before any stipend is released. This
mechanism also functions as distributed professional development,
since recipients acquire skills, such as authoring validators or
converting legacy content, by completing a funded project rather than
attending separate training.

\subsection{Accessibility}
\label{ss:accessibility}

Accessibility work is led by Jeff Kuan of BAWL (Botting Accessibility
with \LaTeX, \url{https://www.bawl-stem.com}), under a \$50,000/year
contract (\$200,000 total), who audits new and existing Ximera content
for WCAG~2.1 Level~AA compliance and maintains the accessibility
sections of the Ximera User Manual. Kuan consults regularly with
Advisory Board member Neil Soiffer of MathCAT (one of three Advisory
Board members receiving a \$1,000/year stipend from the \$3,000/year
Advisory Board line) on mathematical screen-reader compatibility,
including alt text and authoring metadata. Building on the Best
Practices Guide finalized under the current award, the explicit
management goal for this grant is to shift accessibility review from a
single specialist to a distributed practice: consortium authors will
be trained directly in accessible authoring conventions at the annual
Workshop, with Kuan auditing a declining share of total content each
year as that capacity grows. Progress is tracked via audit coverage
and the number of consortium authors completing accessibility
training, reported annually.

\subsection{Modulus Atlas Development}
\label{ss:modulus-atlas}

Modulus Atlas is built by Infonomic under a contract budgeted at
\$100,000 in Year 1, declining to \$70,000, \$70,000, and \$60,000 in
Years 2--4 (\$300,000 total), under the technical direction of Fowler
and the overall direction of Snapp as Project Director. Development
proceeds in four phases tied to the contract's annual milestones:
Year~1, designing the discovery schema and extending Modulus's
existing installation registry to auto-surface newly registered
activities; Year~2, piloting the platform with Uitwiskeling
(\url{https://www.uitwiskeling.be}), the existing Flash-Grant partner
in Belgium; Year~3, public launch alongside initial interoperability
work with PreTeXt, STACK, and WeBWorK; and Year~4, full production
deployment with default, author-overridable discussion threads
attached to every registered activity. Having learned from the
six-month HR delay in initially contracting Infonomic under the
current award, this contract specifies quarterly deliverables rather
than annual ones, giving Snapp and Fowler an earlier signal if
re-scoping or a no-cost extension becomes necessary.
