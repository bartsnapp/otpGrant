\section{Introduction and Background}
Ximera, pronounced ``chimera,'' (\textbf{X}imera:
\textbf{I}nteractive, \textbf{M}athematics, \textbf{E}ducation,
\textbf{R}esources, for \textbf{A}ll) is an open-source platform that
provides tools for authoring and publishing (PDF and Online),
open-source, interactive educational content, such as
textbooks, assessments, and online courses. Ximera serves tens of thousands of students annually, has generated annual savings of more than \$5 million, supports a growing community of authors and instructors, and Ximera servers attract  millions of visitors worldwide.   \textbf{The ultimate goal of this project is to promote sustained student success and savings.} 



The Ximera project depends on four essential parts:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Authoring: Needs to be easy and promote collaboration/remixing.
  \item Content Deployment: Needs to be robust and require minimal interaction from the author.
  \item Grade Data: Required for content to be used in a graded course.
  \item Discoverability and Dissemination: Instructors need to be able to find the content and find support for the content they use.
\end{enumerate}
Thanks to the previous grant, we have solved (or are about to solve) the first three points above. Hence, \textbf{the primary objective of this grant is content discoverability and dissemination.}

His work allows authors to validate against specific forms of algebraic expressions, lists of numbers, and much more. His work provides students with truly innovative ways to communicate mathematics within the confines of online learning.

\paragraph{Students Find Success Through Ximera}

Affordability matters, but student success is the primary goal of this project. 
Authors and instructors choose Ximera for its flexibility \cite{active-learning-osu}.
Ximera lets instructors tailor content to their students' needs, which strengthens
engagement and understanding. \textbf{Research \cite{ximera-calculus} shows that students find
Ximera materials more readable than traditional course materials, and that they
perform as well as students using proprietary textbooks and online homework
systems.}

Research also supports Ximera's impact on student success. OSU researchers
analyzed the relationship between a student's total learning events and their
exam scores \cite{ximera-calculus}. By comparing performance on exams to engagement with Ximera researchers found that more
engagement predicts better exam performance. This points to Ximera's potential
to improve student outcomes in mathematics.


Accessibility is also key to student success. We remain on track to reach WCAG 2.1 AA accessibility by the end of OTP Phase I,
though that work is not yet complete. Even so, in Autumn 2025 and Spring 2026,
several students with vision disabilities successfully completed courses using
Ximera content. This shows that meaningful accessibility already exists on the
Ximera platform, well ahead of our Phase I deadline.

\paragraph{Modulus: Grade Data}\label{modulus}
Large educational institutions use Learning Management Systems (LMS) to manage their grades. Canvas and Blackboard are examples of LMSs. To transfer grades, one needs a Learning Tools Interoperability (LTI) link. LTI is a standard protocol that allows different learning applications to communicate and work together. One of the outcomes of OTP Phase I is the development of a new LTI~1.3 grade pass back via Modulus, a novel assignment-grade database. This is a complex software project that attempts to be as invisible to the users as possible. 
Hence it will provide a seamless and efficient experience for both instructors and students.


 Modulus will allow instructors to easily create and manage Ximera assignments within their LMS using LTI 1.3, while also providing a centralized database for tracking student progress and performance (See Figure \ref{F:modulus}). This integration will enable instructors to access detailed analytics and insights about student engagement and learning outcomes. Students with LTI support do not interact with Modulus directly, for them the experience is from their own LMS.  Moreover, Modulus will also provide alternatives for
    self-learners
    and situations where LTI 1.3 support is not available.

\paragraph{WCAG 2.1 AA Compliance}

Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act requires every state and local government entity, including every public college and university in the country, to bring its web content and mobile applications into conformance with WCAG 2.1 Level AA~\cite{doj2024final}. Large public universities must comply by April 26, 2027, following a one-year extension the Department published in April 2026~\cite{doj2026ifr}. 

All products of this grant and our previous grant must be WCAG 2.1 AA Compliant. As a remedy, we have Jeff Kuan, a math-accessibility specialist as a developer; and Neil Soiffer, the developer of MathCAT, screen reader software for mathematics, on our Advisory Board.  Kuan has made great strides in the accessibility of Ximera, particularly with document navigation and the inclusion of alt-text and MathML `intent' tags. In 2025 at OSU we had several students with visual impairments successfully complete Ximera-based courses.

\paragraph{GitHub: Building Community and Deploying Content}

Our content is primarily released under a Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike (\mbox{CC BY-SA}) license, which allows for universal adaptation and remixing. To integrate existing open educational resources (OER), select materials maintain a Creative Commons Attribution-NonCommercial-ShareAlike (\mbox{CC BY-NC-SA}) license.
The platform's technical infrastructure is similarly open, using targeted licensing to ensure long-term accessibility and transparency:\label{ss:open-license}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\LaTeX\ Document Class:} Licensed under the \LaTeX\ Project Public License (LPPL) to ensure the authoring environment remains freely modifiable and distributable.

\end{itemize}

\section{Statement of Need}\label{ss:need}

\subsection{OER Landscape}
\subsubsection{Barriers to Adoption} National data show that OER awareness has outpaced OER use, and the gap is not closing. In the most recent national faculty survey 59\% of higher-education faculty reported some awareness of OER, yet only 33\% used OER as required course material~\cite{bayview2025}. 

A 2020 multi-institutional study of four large public research universities identifies ``Time constraints'' as the single most-cited obstacle (93\%).  The second most-cited obstacle  is "Finding a comprehensive set of resources" (72\%). ~\cite{elder2020}.

Indeed, when doing a search for linear algebra materials we found hundreds of single-topic videos and lecture notes, dozens of free PDF textbooks, a handful of free textbooks that incorporate some level of interactivity, and none that provide a single integrated structure that combines interactive instructional content, graded assessment (with LMS connectivity), and feedback within a single environment, enabling students to encounter interactive activities as an integral part of the reading experience.

While free online textbooks and online homework systems are widely available, these components are often delivered through separate platforms, making them more difficult, and time-consuming, for instructors to manage and integrate into their LMS structure. 

The ``time constraint'' issue, and the ``lack of comprehensive materials'' barrier, together reveal a multifaceted challenge of \textbf{instructional workflow integration}.

Even when high-quality OER content is available, instructors must often manually create LMS assignments, manage grade synchronization, configure external tools, and provide technical support for students navigating multiple systems. These integration tasks consume valuable instructor time and increase the perceived cost of adoption. Faculty need systems that reduce the time required to discover, evaluate, deploy, assess, and maintain OER materials within existing teaching workflows.

\subsubsection{WCAG 2.1 Compliance} Over the next several years, colleges and universities will face the challenge of bringing existing OER materials into compliance with Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act. Many OER resources for large-enrollment mathematics courses remain distributed primarily as PDF documents and are not currently WCAG 2.1 AA compliant. In contrast, Ximera was designed with accessibility in mind and, following an independent audit by BAWL, is on track to achieve WCAG 2.1 AA conformance by the completion of OTP Phase I.

As institutions seek accessible mathematics solutions, interest in Ximera continues to grow. However, discussions with prospective adopters reveal that many remain unaware of the platform's full capabilities, including its interactive content, assessment tools, and LMS integration. OTP Phase II will address this gap by improving discoverability, community engagement, and adoption pathways, ensuring that institutions can readily identify and implement accessible OER solutions.


\subsection{Current Ximera Landscape}
Ximera was designed to provide complete course content. It provides online interactive textbooks, homework, and can even provide activities for recitation sections. However, discoverability is real issue; a Ximera site might have complete course content, yet navigating to that content might be impossible. Search engines find pages within courses, but the users are unaware of the other content of the course. 
A general search for materials on a common gateway topic returns hundreds of single-topic videos, dozens of free PDF textbooks, and a handful of partially interactive alternatives; Ximera surfaces as one undifferentiated result among that noise rather than as a recognized destination, even where it is the only option that combines instruction, embedded interactives, and auto-graded assessment in a single environment. And because the platform keeps no account of who is using it, the project cannot identify, contact, or support an instructor who has already adopted Ximera material unless that instructor independently reaches out to the Ximera developers.

\subsection{Community of Practice Paradigm}
Repository-based systems largely solve the problem of content discovery. Platforms such as OER Commons, MERLOT, OpenStax, LibreTexts, and the Open Textbook Library make it substantially easier to locate instructional materials than a general web search. However, discovery is only the first step in adoption.

Once content is identified, instructors must still determine how to deploy it within their course structure, integrate it with their LMS, locate or create assessments, manage grade reporting, adapt materials to local needs, and resolve technical issues. Repository platforms generally provide limited support for these downstream instructional workflows.

In addition, repositories typically offer few mechanisms for sustained collaboration among adopters. Instructors may download and use the same resource for years without interacting with one another, sharing improvements, or contributing back to the original project. As a result, valuable implementation knowledge remains distributed across individual institutions rather than accumulating within a shared community.

Ximera already has rich, comprehensive content, Modulus will enable to integrate Ximera content with the instructor's LMS.  During OTP Phase II, we propose to develop mechanisms for discovery of Ximera materials, and sustained collaboration between and amongst authors and instructors.  To this end, we propose to develop ATLAS (\textbf{A}ctivity \textbf{T}opic \textbf{L}ibrary \textbf{A}nd \textbf{S}ocial), an open-source discovery and dissemination platform that serves as the public-facing hub of the Ximera ecosystem and beyond (See Figure~\ref{F:XimeraAtlas}).

  \includegraphics[width=.8\textwidth]{XimeraAtlas.png}

Together, Ximera, Modulus, and ATLAS address the two most significant barriers to OER adoption: ``time constraint'' and ``lack of comprehensive set of resources''. 

Ximera provides the instructional foundation through openly licensed interactive textbooks, embedded activities, and auto-graded assessment. Rather than treating reading and homework as separate experiences, Ximera integrates instruction, practice, and feedback within a single learning environment.

Modulus extends this ecosystem by addressing one of the most persistent challenges identified by instructors: LMS integration. Through grade synchronization and course-management tools, Modulus allows Ximera activities to function within existing institutional workflows, reducing the technical burden associated with adopting new instructional materials. Together, Ximera and Modulus provide a comprehensive pathway from content delivery to assessment and reporting, saving instructors valueable time.

The proposed new component, ATLAS, constitutes the social umbrella for the instructional ecosystem: community, support, and continuous improvement. While Ximera and Modulus provide the instructional and technical infrastructure, ATLAS provides the human infrastructure. Through shared resources, implementation guides, discussion forums, and collaboration tools, ATLAS enables instructors to discover comprehensive materials, exchange ideas, receive technical assistance, and contribute improvements back to the community. In doing so, ATLAS transforms a collection of educational resources into a sustainable and evolving ecosystem of practice.

\section{Project Design}

The primary objective of this grant is improving adoption by alleviating the two most significant barriers to adoption: ``time constraint'' and ``lack of comprehensive materials.``

\subsection{ATLAS: Activity Topic Library And Social}\label{ss:atlas}

The project will develop ATLAS (\textbf{A}ctivity \textbf{T}opic \textbf{L}ibrary \textbf{A}nd \textbf{S}ocial), the public-facing hub of the Ximera ecosystem (See Figure~\ref{atlasSite}). The name is deliberate: like the Titan who held up the heavens, ATLAS supports the weight of the ecosystem behind it; like a geographic atlas, it gives instructors a single place to look something up rather than search for it. All content will be freely visible and open without log-in. ATLAS provides a searchable directory of  activities with grade data connected to a Modulus server, organized by topic,  level, and institution, so that finding materials no longer depends on already knowing someone who uses it.

\end{figure}

What differentiates ATLAS from a repository+social media of choice structure is that ATLAS will be built to answer the specific needs of the Ximera project.
ATLAS will treat each activity less like a file and more like a profile: a topic page that carries its own metadata and, by default, a blog-like discussion thread, and things like implementation notes. This gives an instructor evaluating an unfamiliar activity the same kind of context a colleague's recommendation would, at the scale of the full ecosystem rather than one institution's personal network, directly addressing the search-and-evaluation time-cost identified as the dominant adoption barrier.  

ATLAS servers will be connected to existing Modulus installations. Authors will receive  data on their online activies posted to ATLAS. ATLAS users will be able to easily incorporate ATLAS posts into course materials that they can share with their students and collect grade data. 

The deep software integration of ATLAS manifests across three core instructional workflows (see Figure \ref{F:atlasmap}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Automated Content Indexing via Git:} Because Ximera source documents are hosted on GitHub, ATLAS does not rely on manual form submissions. It actively parses the semantic \LaTeX\ metadata tags from public repositories, automatically indexing new problem sets, interactive applets, and textbook chapters into a searchable national catalog the moment an author pushes code.
    \item \textbf{Curriculum Fork-Tracking:} When an instructor copies or ``forks'' an existing textbook to tailor it to local workforce demands, ATLAS programmatically tracks and visualizes that lineage. This allows prospective adopters to see exactly how a foundational core spine has been mutated by peer institutions (e.g., viewing an OSU Calculus spine alongside a version adapted by Columbus State).
    \item \textbf{Direct LMS Provisioning via Modulus:} ATLAS interfaces directly with the Modulus database engine to streamline adoption mechanics. When an instructor identifies a desired learning module within the ATLAS graphical interface, they can click a unified deployment trigger that instantly communicates with Modulus to provision the necessary \mbox{LTI 1.3} tokens and instantiate the corresponding gradebook columns inside their local LMS instance.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Assessment of Existing Open Educational Resources}   % (a)

Before proposing new development, we assessed the existing open
textbook marketplace at two levels: the architectural level (how
content is delivered and how interactivity scales) and the validation
level (how student responses are checked, relative to industry-leading

\subsubsection{Architectural gaps} At the architectural level, the
current open textbook marketplace shows five recurring gaps: limited
interactive or dynamic content, with many free and open textbooks
restricted to static PDFs or web pages; a lack of integrated online
homework systems, forcing educators who want a free textbook paired
with free homework to choose one or the other; limited content
customization, since most online textbooks do not allow an instructor
to adapt material to their own students; inconsistent ADA compliance
across the marketplace as a whole, leaving many free and open
resources inaccessible to some students with disabilities (a gap
Ximera itself has only recently closed through the accessibility work
described under Absolute Priority~3); and, most consequentially for
scale, a near-universal reliance on server-side interactivity.
Table~\ref{tab:calc-comparison} summarizes this last gap among the
leading free calculus textbooks.

Server-side interactivity requires a central server to mediate every
student interaction. When the number of students attempting to
interact with that server grows large, server-side solutions become
difficult and expensive to scale; Active Calculus, the only one of
these peer texts to offer dynamic content at all, does so through a
server-side architecture. Ximera, by contrast, performs all
interactivity, answer checking, graphing, and feedback on the
student's own computer (Section~\ref{ss:LaTeX}); the central server's
role is limited to serving static content. This is why Ximera has been
able to support MOOCs with tens of thousands of simultaneous
enrollments and OSU's large coordinated calculus sections without the
server-capacity problems that constrain server-side competitors. At
OSU and UF, Ximera-deployed courses already meet or exceed every other
open resource across all five categories in
Table~\ref{tab:calc-comparison}; the task this project addresses is
bringing that same standard to every Ximera course, at every
consortium institution.

\subsection{Monitoring Impact on Instruction, Outcomes, and Costs}     % (b)
Instructional impact is monitored at two levels, mirroring the activity-score and course-score distinction built into Modulus. \subsection{Sustaining and Updating Materials Beyond the Funded Period}% (d)
Open access to content is permanent and does not depend on this grant. All Ximera materials are released under CC BY-SA  and hosted on public GitHub repositories. Nothing below changes this; the question this section addresses is how the people and infrastructure that keep content current and discoverable,  continue once federal funding ends in Year 4.
Our model for ATLAS, Uitwiskeling, sustains a small, dedicated staff through reader subscriptions rather than by charging for access to mathematics itself; we adopt that same principle, with the activities Atlas indexes remaining free and the project instead monetizing a service layered on top of discovery. Our primary service is direct, one-on-one access to expertise: once Atlas is surfacing Ximera activities publicly, an instructor or department will be able to discover an activity for free, then opt into a paid subscription connecting them with a project-affiliated author or experienced instructor for direct consultation on implementing that specific activity in their own classroom.

We prioritize one-on-one consultation for three reasons grounded elsewhere in this narrative. First, it draws on a resource the project has already built and validated: a deep bench of Flash-Grant-funded authors and instructors trained across ten prior Ximera Workshops, who have collectively completed roughly 80 funded projects. Second, it addresses the actual adoption barrier identified in our Statement of Need, faculty time and confidence, rather than a lack of written material; the Ximera User Manual  already cover the latter at no cost. Third, it preserves the direct, personal relationship between authors and adopting instructors that has driven Ximera's organic growth from two institutions to fifteen over the past decade, rather than substituting a written product for it.
Pedagogical companion materials, as a secondary, scaling mechanism. As one-on-one consultations accumulate, recurring questions and implementation patterns will be documented and offered as optional, lower-cost written companion materials, teaching notes, common-misconception guides, suggested pacing, in the spirit of Uitwiskeling's own "Onder De Loep" ("Under the Magnifying Glass") article column. This tier exists to scale the value of consultations already taking place, not to replace them, and will only be developed for a given activity once consultation volume justifies it.

Managed Modulus hosting (fallback, equity-safeguarded). Because Modulus is licensed AGPL, any institution may self-host a Modulus server at no cost in perpetuity; this is a structural commitment, not a transitional one. A lower-priority option under consideration is a fee for a centrally hosted, fully managed instance, for institutions that prefer not to run their own server, an ``open core'' pattern common among open-source infrastructure projects. We treat this option cautiously and would only pursue it with built-in fee waivers for under-resourced and minority-serving institutions, preserving the equity gains documented under Performance Measure 6.

Governance and timeline. Snapp and Fowler will explore appropriate organizational structures, including fiscal sponsorship or formal nonprofit status, for administering the consultation subscription. We will pilot it in Year 2 alongside the existing Atlas pilot, introduce companion materials opportunistically as consultation volume warrants, and report uptake and revenue in the APR alongside, with the goal of a piloted, evaluated sustainability model in place by Year 4.
