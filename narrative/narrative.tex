\section{Introduction}
Ximera, pronounced ``chimera,'' (\textbf{X}imera:
\textbf{I}nteractive, \textbf{M}athematics, \textbf{E}ducation,
\textbf{R}esources, for \textbf{A}ll) is an open-source platform that
provides tools for authoring and publishing (PDF and Online),
open-source, interactive educational content, such as
textbooks, assessments, and online courses. Ximera serves tens of thousands of students annually, has generated annual savings of more than \$5 million, supports a growing community of authors and instructors, and Ximera servers attract  millions of visitors worldwide.   \textbf{The ultimate goal of this project is to promote sustained student success and savings.} The Ximera project depends on four essential parts:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Authoring: Needs to be easy and promote collaboration/remixing.
  \item Content Deployment: Needs to be robust and invisible to the author.
  \item Grade data from: Required to be used in a graded course.
  \item Discoverability and Dissemination: Instructors need to be able to find the content.
\end{itemize}
With our previous funding, we have solved (or are about to solve) the first three points above. Hence, \textbf{the primary objective of this grant is content discoverability and dissemination.} 



\subsection*{Past Awards and Current Funding} 
The Ximera Project is funded 2024--2026 (with no other external funding) by a \$2,125,000 Open Textbooks Pilot Program grant. We will refer to this as OTP Phase I funding. In the past, the Ximera Project has also received support from NSF Grant DUE-1245433 (\$180,000), the Shuttleworth Foundation (\$5,000), the Affordable Learning Exchange at The Ohio State University (OSU) (\$16,000), and the OSU Department of Mathematics.

\subsubsection{Ximera is Interactive} Higher levels of student engagement lead to better learning
outcomes. An analysis of student interactions with a mathematics
digital textbook, while learning a new topic, by Reinhold et

higher engaged clusters reaching higher outcomes.'' Ximera's auto-graded prompts give students
instant feedback on the correctness of their answers, allowing them to
correct mistakes and solidify their understanding.

such as \textit{Desmos}~\cite{desmos} or \textit{GeoGebra}~\cite{geogebra} directly into Ximera
documents. This gives students multiple ways to engage

\subsubsection{Students Find Success Through Ximera}

While affordability is an important benefit, student success remains the primary goal of the project.
Authors and instructors use Ximera because of its flexibility \cite{active-learning-osu}. Ximera 
empowers university instructors to tailor content based on the
specific needs of their students, enhancing student engagement and
understanding.  

Moreover, Ximera's ability to provide backup materials in the form of
PDFs of the online course materials ensures continuity of learning
even in the face of technical difficulties or internet disruptions
\cite{active-learning-osu}.  

Research also supports the effectiveness of Ximera for student success.  Specifically,
researchers at OSU analyzed the correlation between the total number of
learning events for a student and their exam scores
\cite{ximera-calculus}. The study found a significant relationship
between increased learning events and improved exam performance. More
specifically, a simple linear regression was used to predict a
student's total exam score based on the logarithm of the number of
learning events recorded for that student. The results revealed a
positive correlation, suggesting that higher engagement, as measured
by learning events, contributes to improved academic outcomes. These
findings highlight the potential of Ximera to enhance student success
in mathematics courses.
Together, these findings suggest that Ximera advances both components of the project's mission: reducing educational costs while supporting student success.




\subsection{Institutions}


\subsubsection{Modulus: Grade Data}

Large educational institutions use Learning Management Systems (LMS) to manage their grades. Canvas and Blackboard are examples of LMSs. To transfer grades, one needs a Learning Tools Interoperability (LTI) link. LTI is a standard protocol that allows different learning applications to communicate and work together. One of the outcomes of our current work is the development of a new LTI~1.3 connection via Modulus, a novel assignment-grade database. This new connection provides a seamless and efficient experience for both instructors and students.

Modulus is being developed by Infonomic under the direction of Snapp and Fowler. At the time of this writing, it is being tested in one of OSU's Canvas installation. Modulus will allow instructors to easily create and manage Ximera assignments within their LMS, while also providing a centralized database for tracking student progress and performance. This integration will enable instructors to access detailed analytics and insights about student engagement and learning outcomes, ultimately supporting the goal of promoting sustained student success (See Figure \ref{F:modulus}). Modulus will also provide alternatives for
    self-learners
    and situations where LTI 1.3 support is not available.

\subsection{Instructors}

Instructors (who are not authors) can freely use any Ximera materials simply by sharing the materials' URL.
Since Ximera courses are built in a modular fashion, these URLs might be from various sources. Modulus will all these instructors to get consolidated grade data even when they choose content from various Ximera sources. 
Instructor use of Ximera falls into three categories:

\subsubsection{PDF Materials}
There are a number of Ximera courses that are not yet deployed
online. Their materials are currently only in PDF form. Instructors in
this category are usually in a development phase of their Ximera
course. The PDFs produced allow incremental development of the
materials while still being used and tested in the classroom. 

\subsubsection{Online Materials without LMS Integration}
At other institutions using Ximera, instructors can use Ximera
materials, and have their students complete various Ximera
assignments. However, data access is prohibitively difficult for most
instructors in this category; Modulus solves this issue.


\subsubsection{Online Materials with LMS Integration} At 
OSU and UF, instructors of large classes and a few others have Ximera
assignments directly integrated into the Learning Management System
(LMS). For these instructors, Ximera works seamlessly and invisibly.




As Ximera authors create more materials with interactive features in mind, instructors increasingly have options to incorporate discussions, group work, labs, and other forms of interactive learning into their classrooms. For example, the Differential Equations text utilizes spreadsheet labs that include embedded videos and links to sample spreadsheets; Linear Algebra includes many geometric explorations and links to online coding exercises, while Precalculus by Stitz and Zeager, currently being converted to Ximera, boasts connections between the concrete and the abstract that can be used for classroom discussion or to encourage independent reading  (See Figure \ref{F:llama}).

\subsection{Authors}
\subsubsection{Familiar Writing Tools}
With Ximera, authors use the document preparation system
\LaTeX\ to create content.  \LaTeX\ is a markup language created in the 1980s. It provides a
simplified way to produce documents by separating the content and
structure from the visual formatting. 
 While this
system may seem obscure to the layperson, it is enormously popular in
mathematics and other STEM fields. For example, the preprint server
arXiv hosts more than 2 million papers, nearly all of which were
authored in \LaTeX. This narrative was authored in \LaTeX. As a result, many potential authors already have
course materials written in \LaTeX, making it a natural authoring
environment for STEM content. In May 2024, the platform achieved a critical scaling milestone by publishing the \texttt{ximera} document class to the Comprehensive \TeX{} Archive Network (CTAN). Now natively bundled within standard global distributions like \TeX{} Live and MiK\TeX{}, any educator worldwide can instantly begin authoring interactive STEM materials via \texttt{\textbackslash documentclass\{ximera\}} with zero local configuration. This integration shifts Ximera from an isolated campus utility to a permanent, globally available piece of open educational infrastructure.

Ximera allows authors to use a single \LaTeX\ source file to

\subsubsection{GitHub: Building Community and Deploying Content}

Our content is primarily released under a Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike (\mbox{CC BY-SA}) license, which allows for universal adaptation and remixing. To integrate existing open educational resources (OER), select materials maintain a Creative Commons Attribution-NonCommercial-ShareAlike (\mbox{CC BY-NC-SA}) license.
The platform's technical infrastructure is similarly open, using targeted licensing to ensure long-term accessibility and transparency:\label{ss:open-license}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\LaTeX\ Document Class:} Licensed under the \LaTeX\ Project Public License (LPPL) to ensure the authoring environment remains freely modifiable and distributable.
    \item \textbf{Ximera Server:} Powered by code licensed under the GNU General Public License (GPL) to protect user freedom and collaborative development.
    \item \textbf{Modulus Assignment Database:} Licensed under the GNU Affero General Public License (AGPL). This network-specific license guarantees that the source code remains accessible and transparent even when run strictly as a cloud-hosted service.
\end{itemize}



All of these files are hosted on GitHub, a web-based platform for version control and collaborative
software development. As a result, authors can easily collaborate,
share materials, and contribute improvements. If a user (student, instructor, or author) encounters issues with the content or platform performance, GitHub provides straightforward ways to report them.
For example, the GitHub repository for The Ohio State University's calculus sequence
has 41 contributors, authors have resolved 419 issues in the course materials, and the content been `forked' 460 times (each fork
represents a copy of the course materials that can be adapted and
extended by another instructor or author).

\subsubsection{Students}

The most significant benefit for students has been the expansion and enhancement of Ximera materials. Since the beginning of the grant period, we have supported the development and improvement of additional textbooks, including Linear Algebra, and Differential Equations. Our most recent undertaking is the conversion of the popular OER Precalculus textbook by Stitz and Zeager to the Ximera platform, with completion anticipated by autumn 2026.

Converting existing OER materials to Ximera allows us to incorporate auto-graded exercises, embedded interactives, and other Ximera features, enhancing these resources for today's students. Continued funding will allow us to build on this progress by expanding the library of Ximera materials, updating existing content, and bringing additional open educational resources to the platform.

\subsubsection{Instructors}

Current funding has allowed us to make significant strides in improving the instructor experience. The most notable advancement has been the development of the Modulus tool, which enables Ximera assignments to pass student grades directly to a learning management system (LMS) (see Section~???). Modulus is being developed by Infonomic under the direction of Snapp and Fowler. In its current implementation, Modulus successfully passes grades to Canvas at The Ohio State University.

As testing at additional institutions and on other learning management systems (e.g., Blackboard and Brightspace) is completed, instructors will be able to integrate Ximera assignments into their existing LMS workflows and record student progress automatically.

Future funding will allow us to expand the role of Modulus in supporting instructors. One proposed enhancement, Modulus Atlas, would not only allow instructors to assign and link Ximera materials of their choice through the LMS, but also provide tools for discovering other relevant Ximera content to incorporate into their courses.

\subsubsection{Authors}

While authoring Ximera documents in \LaTeX\ has always been straightforward, the deployment process prior to 2024 required significant technical expertise. Current funding has allowed us to substantially improve the author experience. The following tools and resources have simplified the process of deploying Ximera content:

\begin{itemize}
\item A comprehensive user manual for authors was developed in 2025.
\item GitHub repositories containing templates for authors were created. These templates include the necessary structure and files to get started with creating and automatically deploying Ximera content. 
The repository located at
\begin{center}
\url{https://github.com/ximeraProject/ximeraFirstSteps}
\end{center}
provides a starting point for authors to create their own Ximera content.
\item Deployment workflows using GitHub Codespaces and Overleaf have been developed.
\end{itemize}

The last item, in particular, automates much of the deployment process. After the initial setup is complete, deployment occurs automatically behind the scenes, allowing authors to focus on content creation rather than technical implementation.

Future funding will allow us to further streamline and automate deployment workflows, reducing technical barriers for new authors and making Ximera content creation accessible to a broader community of educators.

\subsection{Data Demonstrating the Issue, Challenge, or Opportunity}

National data confirm that the barrier to open-resource adoption is not the absence of good material, but faculty's ability to find, evaluate, and trust it. In the most recent national faculty survey, conducted in April 2025 with 3,447 respondents across all 50 states, 59\% of higher education faculty reported some awareness of OER, but 30\% remained entirely unaware, and only 33\% used OER as a required course material, the highest rate recorded in the survey's twelve-year history, but still meaning two out of three faculty do not~\cite{bayview2025}. Critically, the same survey's open-ended responses show that awareness alone does not translate to adoption: faculty who are favorably disposed toward OER repeatedly cited the time required to search for and evaluate suitable material as the factor slowing their own rollout~\cite{bayview2025}. This is a discoverability problem, not an awareness or quality problem.

The financial stakes of closing this gap are well established nationally. Students spent an average of \$341 on required course materials in 2024-25~\cite{nacs2025}, a figure that, while declining as free and digital alternatives spread, still represents real, avoidable cost for tens of millions of students enrolled in gateway courses each year. Within mathematics specifically, the architectural gaps already identified in this proposal (Table 3, Section 3.1) show why so few existing free resources close this gap on their own: most free calculus texts lack dynamic content, integrated homework, or content customization, and the one peer text that offers dynamic content does so through a server-side architecture that does not scale to large enrollments.

Ximera has already demonstrated that these gaps are solvable at meaningful scale: 45,706 students at fifteen institutions saved an estimated \$5.48 million in the 2025 calendar year alone (Table 1), and Ximera-supported Calculus I sections recorded an 18\% DFW rate against a national benchmark of 27\% (Section 7). But set against a national faculty population in which two-thirds still do not use OER as required material, this is a small fraction of the addressable need. The opportunity is not to prove that high-quality, interactive, free materials can work, that is established, but to close the gap between their existence and their discovery by the faculty who would use them.


A binding federal deadline sharpens this opportunity considerably. Under Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act, the Department of Justice's 2024 final rule requires every state and local government entity, including every public college and university in the country, more than 1,600 institutions nationally~\cite{nces-public}, to bring its web content and mobile applications into conformance with WCAG 2.1 Level AA~\cite{doj2024final}. Large public entities, a category that includes The Ohio State University and most of this consortium's public partner institutions, must comply by April 26, 2027, following a one-year extension the Department published in April 2026~\cite{doj2026ifr}. Static PDF course materials, the format in which most free and open textbooks are currently distributed, including the architectural gap already identified in Section 3.1, do not meet this standard on their own. Ximera, audited against WCAG 2.1 Level AA from the outset by BAWL (Section 6.9), already does. This converts an existing project strength into a time-bound national need: institutions that must solve this compliance problem before April 2027 anyway can solve it by adopting already-compliant Ximera materials instead of separately remediating their current PDFs.



\subsection{Nature and Magnitude of Gaps and How the Project Addresses Them}

The national data above point to a three-part funnel failure, and the Ximera project is now positioned, for the first time, to address all three within a single grant period.

\textbf{Faculty are often unaware that suitable material exists.} Even strong, free, interactive materials like Ximera's remain invisible to faculty outside the consortium's existing network unless someone happens to share a direct link. With no link, no awareness; with no awareness, no adoption, regardless of quality.

\textbf{Even aware faculty face a search-and-evaluation cost that suppresses adoption.} This is the barrier faculty themselves identify most often~\cite{bayview2025}, and it is distinct from awareness: a faculty member can know OER exists in general while still lacking an efficient way to find material matched to their specific course, institution type, and student population.

\textbf{Successful adoptions, once they happen, have historically gone uncounted and unsupported.} Because Ximera content requires no sign-up or login, the project has had no reliable way to know who is using it unless an instructor independently reaches out, and no mechanism to pass grades into an institution's LMS without manual, instructor-built workarounds.

Hence, the challenge addressed by this project is not the creation of additional content. It is ensuring that existing and newly developed resources are discoverable, maintainable, reliable, and capable of supporting student success over time.

This project addresses each gap directly. Modulus Atlas (Section 6.2), modeled on the Flemish portal Uitwiskeling, closes the awareness and search-cost gaps by surfacing every registered Ximera activity to instructors with no prior connection to the consortium, automatically and continuously, rather than depending on a faculty member's existing network. Because faculty cite \emph{time}, not skepticism, as the binding constraint~\cite{bayview2025}, Atlas is paired with a direct, one-on-one expert consultation service (Section 4.4) rather than written documentation alone, addressing the barrier at its actual source. Modulus's LTI 1.3 connection (Section 1.2) closes the third gap structurally, giving every adopting institution a path to grade integration and giving the project, for the first time, real usage data rather than self-reported estimates.

This need is particularly acute in high-enrollment gateway courses, where even modest barriers to adoption affect thousands of students per institution per year, and where the national spending and awareness figures above show that most of the addressable population has not yet been reached. To convert Ximera's demonstrated, but still narrow, impact into impact at national scale, the consortium must pair high-quality open resources with the discovery, integration, and support infrastructure this project builds.

\section{Project Design}

The consortium will promote student success through a comprehensive strategy that combines open resource adoption, implementation support, continuous improvement, and long-term sustainability.

\subsection{Flash Grants for Local Adoption and Improvement}\label{ss:fg}

To accelerate adoption across consortium institutions, the project will establish a competitive Flash Grant program. Small, rapid-turnaround awards will provide faculty and departments with targeted support to adopt, adapt, or expand the use of Modulus-enabled open educational resources in high-enrollment courses.

Flash Grants will support activities such as course redesign, resource adaptation, accessibility improvements, assessment development, and implementation evaluation. By reducing the local costs of adoption, the program will expand the use of open resources while generating new examples, adaptations, and evidence that can be shared across the consortium.

\subsection{Modulus Atlas: Discovery, Dissemination, and Sustainability}

The project will develop Modulus Atlas, an open discovery and dissemination platform that serves as the public-facing hub of the Modulus ecosystem. Atlas will provide a searchable directory of Modulus-instrumented learning activities organized by academic discipline and educational sector, enabling instructors to identify relevant resources and implementation models.

Atlas will integrate with the existing Modulus registry infrastructure to automatically surface newly registered activities, helping ensure that innovations developed through the project remain visible and reusable. The platform will also host documentation, implementation guides, case studies, and evidence of impact, reducing barriers to adoption and strengthening long-term sustainability. By transforming discovery from a manual process into an ongoing platform function, Atlas will support continued growth and use of project resources well beyond the grant period.

\subsection{Expanding and Integrating Existing OER}

Consistent with the Open Textbook Pilot requirement to assess and build upon existing resources before creating new ones, the consortium will systematically identify, evaluate, and integrate high-quality OER from across the broader educational ecosystem. Rather than duplicating existing work, the project will prioritize adaptation, enhancement, and interoperability wherever appropriate.

Existing open textbooks, ancillary materials, simulations, assessments, and learning activities will be reviewed for alignment with project objectives and incorporated into the Modulus ecosystem when suitable. Resources integrated through the project will be enhanced with Modulus instrumentation, accessibility improvements, implementation guidance, and evidence-based instructional supports. This approach maximizes efficiency, accelerates adoption, and expands the reach of proven open resources while creating a more cohesive ecosystem for faculty and students.

\subsection{Monitoring Impact and Improving Student Outcomes}

The consortium will use Modulus analytics, institutional data, and implementation reporting to track adoption, student savings, instructional practices, and learning outcomes across participating institutions. Measures will include resource utilization, course completion, withdrawal rates, academic performance, faculty adoption, and direct student cost savings.

Findings will be used to identify evidence-based practices associated with successful OER implementation and student achievement. These practices will be documented through Atlas and disseminated through consortium workshops, professional development activities, publications, and conference presentations.

\subsection{Sustainability Beyond the Grant Period}

Sustainability is built directly into the project design. Modulus Atlas provides a durable dissemination infrastructure, while the Flash Grant program establishes a replicable model for supporting ongoing local innovation. By combining discoverability, implementation support, evidence generation, and community engagement, the project creates a self-reinforcing ecosystem that will continue to expand the adoption and impact of open educational resources after federal funding concludes.

Through these integrated activities, the project will achieve substantial student cost savings, increase adoption of open educational resources in high-enrollment courses, improve educational outcomes, and create a sustainable framework for continued growth and innovation.

\section{Evaluation Plan}



Our evaluation plan reuses a data collection and benchmarking
methodology that has already operated successfully for three years
under the current FIPSE award (P116T230012), giving us confidence both
in its feasibility and in our ability to interpret year-over-year
trends consistently across the two awards.
 
\subsection{Personnel.} Evaluation will be conducted by Elizabeth
Miller, an Instructional Designer in OSU's Mathematics and Statistics
Learning Center, and Carolyn Johns, the Center's Interim Director.
Both have served as the project's evaluators since 2024 and are
budgeted at a level of effort proportional to the scope of work in
each year of this grant (Budget Narrative). Their continuity across
the two awards ensures consistent measurement definitions, survey
instruments, and data handling from one grant period into the next, a
key feasibility advantage over standing up a new evaluation function
from scratch.
 
\subsection{Data collection.} Each semester, Ms.~Miller and
Dr.~Johns distribute structured surveys to all known Ximera-adopting
instructors and institutions, collecting enrollment counts, course
completions, failure/withdrawal rates, instructor counts, and
section-level adoption data. These survey responses are cross-checked
against institutional and instructor-level reporting where available,
and compiled into the project's longitudinal dataset at the end of
each semester and reported annually.
 
\subsection{External benchmarking.} Rather than relying solely on
internal targets, our methodology benchmarks Ximera-supported outcomes
against published, peer-reviewed national data wherever possible. For
the calculus sequence, we compare DFW (drop/fail/withdraw) rates and
average final grades against Bressoud et al.'s national study of the
calculus sequence, \emph{The Calculus Sequence: Catalyzing Change in
the First Two Years} and related work on the strange role of calculus
in the United States. Under the current award, this benchmarking
showed Ximera-supported Calculus~I sections at an 18\% DFW rate
against a national benchmark of 27\%, and an average GPA of 2.66
against a national benchmark of 2.52. We will continue this
externally-anchored comparison rather than relying only on
internally-defined targets, which strengthens the rigor and relevance
of our outcome claims.

Ximera and Modulus allow instructors and institutions to manage their own materials.

Since Ximera uses open licensing and is hosted publicly on GitHub, it naturally functions as a collaborative ecosystem. \subsubsection{Modulus Atlas}\label{par:modulus-atlas} Discovery is the central bottleneck for any
open educational ecosystem: valuable materials remain invisible to the
people who would most benefit from them, and adoption depends on
personal networks. To solve this, Infonomic will build \emph{Modulus

full Ximera ecosystem, activities, topics, grade levels, and

architecture, the Atlas automatically surfaces and announces newly
registered activities, including a default, auto-generated discussion
for each activity that authors may override with their own commentary.
We will model Modulus Atlas on \emph{Uitwiskeling}

that proven model to the Ximera ecosystem, turning dissemination from
a periodic, manual effort into an ongoing, largely automatic function
of the platform.

deliberate interoperability with peer OER projects, allow the

\section{Absolute Priority 2: Addressing Gaps in the Open Textbook Marketplace and Bringing Solutions to Scale}
% Comprehensive plan covering all six elements:
\subsection{Assessment of Existing Open Educational Resources}   % (a)

Before proposing new development, we assessed the existing open
textbook marketplace at two levels: the architectural level (how
content is delivered and how interactivity scales) and the validation
level (how student responses are checked, relative to industry-leading
commercial platforms).

\subsubsection{Architectural gaps} At the architectural level, the
current open textbook marketplace shows five recurring gaps: limited
interactive or dynamic content, with many free and open textbooks
restricted to static PDFs or web pages; a lack of integrated online
homework systems, forcing educators who want a free textbook paired
with free homework to choose one or the other; limited content
customization, since most online textbooks do not allow an instructor
to adapt material to their own students; inconsistent ADA compliance
across the marketplace as a whole, leaving many free and open
resources inaccessible to some students with disabilities (a gap
Ximera itself has only recently closed through the accessibility work
described under Absolute Priority~3); and, most consequentially for
scale, a near-universal reliance on server-side interactivity.
Table~\ref{tab:calc-comparison} summarizes this last gap among the
leading free calculus textbooks.

Server-side interactivity requires a central server to mediate every
student interaction. When the number of students attempting to
interact with that server grows large, server-side solutions become
difficult and expensive to scale; Active Calculus, the only one of
these peer texts to offer dynamic content at all, does so through a
server-side architecture. Ximera, by contrast, performs all
interactivity, answer checking, graphing, and feedback on the
student's own computer (Section~\ref{ss:LaTeX}); the central server's
role is limited to serving static content. This is why Ximera has been
able to support MOOCs with tens of thousands of simultaneous
enrollments and OSU's large coordinated calculus sections without the
server-capacity problems that constrain server-side competitors. At
OSU and UF, Ximera-deployed courses already meet or exceed every other
open resource across all five categories in
Table~\ref{tab:calc-comparison}; the task this project addresses is
bringing that same standard to every Ximera course, at every
consortium institution.


\subsubsection{Ximera is Client Side}

All of the
interactivity provided by Ximera happens on the student's
computer. This means we can serve a much higher volume of students
than our competitors that have server side interaction. We demonstrate
our ability to handle large volumes of students with our experience
with MOOCs and OSU's large calculus classes. Server side solutions
cannot provide the same experience when there are tens-of-thousands of
students. (See Figure \ref{F:server})

\subsubsection{Answer Verification and Computer Algebra System}

\textit{Math-expressions} is a JavaScript library that can be used for parsing and working with mathematical expressions. 
In theory (and in
practice), a computer algebra system cannot always validate correct
answers as correct, and incorrect answers as incorrect.  This is due to Richardson's theorem which states that 
equality is undecidable between certain types of functions. \cite{richardson}. 
A popular validation technique is to check for equality at random \textit{real} points
\cite{fisher}. This and some additional techniques are summarized by Chris Sangwin \cite{sangwin}.

In contrast, math-expressions uses complex numbers to validate
answers (see Figure \ref{F:validator}). This seems to work quite well.  One metric is the STACK
project \cite{stack}, which has produced a wide variety of pairs of
expressions which should be adjudicated as equal, and pairs which
should be adjudicated as different.  These form the basis for unit
tests of math-expressions.  Out of more than five thousand test cases,
math-expressions fails on just a handful.

\begin{figure}
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.8\textwidth]{answerValidator.png}
  \caption{Different versions of the correct answer are accepted as correct.}
  \label{F:validator}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Validation-level gaps} Beyond architecture, we conducted a
systematic comparison of Ximera's answer-validation and authoring
capabilities against the leading commercial mathematics homework
platforms. Here by `validator' we mean a given online homework system's ability to detect a correct answer from an incorrect answer and vice versa. we evaluated each platform: Pearson MyLab Math, McGraw Hill ALEKS, Cengage WebAssign,
Hawkes Learning, and MyOpenMath/IMathAS. Seven dimensions were analyzed: standard response validation, algebraic and
functional submissions, graphing, randomization, factored-form
validation, targeted feedback, and custom-validation authoring.

For ordinary response types, numeric entry, text entry, multiple
choice, and select-all, the major platforms are broadly comparable,
and Ximera's support is on par with industry leaders. The more
consequential differences emerge in mathematically richer territory.
Ximera's default algebraic validator is unusually robust because its
equivalence check samples many complex-plane points, motivated by the
identity theorem, rather than relying only on a small fixed set of
real-number substitutions.


Ximera also offers additional flexibility by exposing an optional
author-written JavaScript validation pathway as well as one using the open-source computer algebra system SAGE.
These powerful tools allow Ximera to validate novel mathematical forms that simply would not be possible with other systems.

\subsection{Promoting and Tracking Use; Projected Direct Cost Savings} % (a)


\subsubsection{Promoting Use} Flash Grants (Section~\ref{ss:fg}) fund the creation and improvement of activities; Modulus Atlas (Section 6.10) makes those activities discoverable to instructors with no prior connection to the consortium; and Paul Zachlin's direct outreach (Section~\ref{ss:fg}) converts that visibility into adoption. Atlas surfaces what Flash Grants produce, and dissemination carries it the rest of the way.

Tracking use relies on two data streams. The first, semester surveys administered by Elizabeth Miller and Carolyn Johns and cross-checked against institutional reporting, has operated successfully since 2024 (Section 7). The second is new to this grant period: once Modulus is fully deployed, anonymized, activity-level usage data exported directly from the Modulus database (Section 6.10) will let us observe actual student engagement and completion, rather than depending entirely on instructor self-report, for any institution using the LTI 1.3 connection.

Projected direct cost savings follow the same conservative, known-enrollment-only methodology used to establish our baseline (Table 1, Section 7), valuing each avoided textbook purchase at $120. Applied to the Year 1–4 targets in Table 4, this methodology projects total annual student savings growing from a $5.48 million baseline to between $9.6 and $12 million by Year 4. We report this conservative figure, rather than an extrapolated one, because it remains defensible under external scrutiny; actual savings, which include enrollments we are not independently aware of, are very likely higher.






\subsection{Monitoring Impact on Instruction, Outcomes, and Costs}     % (b)
Instructional impact is monitored at two levels, mirroring the activity-score and course-score distinction built into Modulus (Section 1.2). Activity-level data gives instructors and the project team granular visibility into which assignments are being used and how effectively; course-level data, computed by the same formula instructors use for final grades, supports our headline outcome measures (PM3, PM4).
Outcomes are evaluated against an external, peer-reviewed benchmark rather than an internally defined target, as described in Section 7: DFW rates and average grades in Ximera-supported sections are compared annually to Bressoud et al.'s published national calculus data. This benchmarking will expand to additional gateway courses, Precalculus and Linear Algebra, as comparable national data becomes available, giving us a year-over-year picture of whether Ximera-supported instruction is keeping pace with national trends, not only whether our own numbers are rising.



Costs are monitored through the same survey and usage data described in Section 4.1. We additionally track a measure not previously reported under the current award: the marginal cost of supporting each newly adopting institution, calculated as Flash Grant and Dissemination Specialist effort divided by new institutions onboarded that year. As Modulus Atlas matures into a self-service discovery channel, we expect this marginal cost to decline, a trend we will report annually as evidence that the dissemination model scales sublinearly with adoption.

\subsection{Evidence-Based Practices and Dissemination}                % (c)

Identifying evidence-based practices. Findings from the monitoring in Section ??, which activities, course designs, and implementation patterns correlate with lower DFW rates, higher remixing activity, or faster instructor onboarding, are reviewed by Snapp, Fowler, and the evaluation team at the quarterly check-ins described in the Management Plan (Section ??). \subsection{Sustaining and Updating Materials Beyond the Funded Period}% (d)
Open access to content is permanent and does not depend on this grant. All Ximera materials are released under CC BY-SA (Section 1.4) and hosted on public GitHub repositories. Nothing below changes this; the question this section addresses is how the people and infrastructure that keep content current and discoverable, the roles funded under Sections 6.6–6.10, continue once federal funding ends in Year 4.
Modeled on Uitwiskeling, with one-on-one consultation as the first choice. Our model for Modulus Atlas, Uitwiskeling (Section 2.4), sustains a small, dedicated staff through reader subscriptions rather than by charging for access to mathematics itself; we adopt that same principle, with the activities Atlas indexes remaining free and the project instead monetizing a service layered on top of discovery. Our primary service is direct, one-on-one access to expertise: once Atlas is surfacing Ximera activities publicly, an instructor or department will be able to discover an activity for free, then opt into a paid subscription connecting them with a project-affiliated author or experienced instructor for direct consultation on implementing that specific activity in their own classroom.

We prioritize one-on-one consultation for three reasons grounded elsewhere in this narrative. First, it draws on a resource the project has already built and validated: a deep bench of Flash-Grant-funded authors and instructors trained across ten prior Ximera Workshops (Section 3.5), who have collectively completed roughly 80 funded projects (Section 6.8). Second, it addresses the actual adoption barrier identified in our Statement of Need (Section 5), faculty time and confidence, rather than a lack of written material; the Ximera User Manual and Best Practices Guide (Section 6.9) already cover the latter at no cost. Third, it preserves the direct, personal relationship between authors and adopting instructors that has driven Ximera's organic growth from two institutions to fifteen over the past decade (Section 2.1), rather than substituting a written product for it.
Pedagogical companion materials, as a secondary, scaling mechanism. As one-on-one consultations accumulate, recurring questions and implementation patterns will be documented and offered as optional, lower-cost written companion materials, teaching notes, common-misconception guides, suggested pacing, in the spirit of Uitwiskeling's own "Under the Magnifying Glass" column. This tier exists to scale the value of consultations already taking place, not to replace them, and will only be developed for a given activity once consultation volume justifies it.

Managed Modulus hosting (fallback, equity-safeguarded). Because Modulus is licensed AGPL (Section 1.4), any institution may self-host a Modulus server at no cost in perpetuity; this is a structural commitment, not a transitional one. A lower-priority option under consideration is a fee for a centrally hosted, fully managed instance, for institutions that prefer not to run their own server, an "open core" pattern common among open-source infrastructure projects. We treat this option cautiously and would only pursue it with built-in fee waivers for under-resourced and minority-serving institutions, preserving the equity gains documented under Performance Measure 6.

Governance and timeline. Snapp and Fowler will explore appropriate organizational structures, including fiscal sponsorship or formal nonprofit status, for administering the consultation subscription. We will pilot it in Year 2 alongside the existing Atlas/Uitwiskeling pilot (Section 6.10), introduce companion materials opportunistically as consultation volume warrants, and report uptake and revenue in the APR alongside the other Section 6.10 milestones, with the goal of a piloted, evaluated sustainability model in place by Year 4.
